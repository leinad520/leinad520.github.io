Long story short, I started arbitraging inefficient crypto markets (Medium: How I Made $15,000 / Day as a Fresh College Grad) from our small NYC bedroom. This and other early investments made me a millionaire by my mid 20s. I was proud of myself. But then I quickly felt hollow. The best in the space were engaged in a movement, not just profiting off market inefficiencies. That was how I found myself in the Bay Area in 2018, at age 24, writing a book and making a crypto wiki to help people cut through the noise and understand why this technology mattered. Hundreds of projects with technical jargon were popping up every day, where even someone full-time like myself would get lost in it all.

To me, crypto is an equalizing force - an open industry where people are judged on merit, creativity and contribution rather than status, and a technology that enables people to more equitably share in the wealth that innovation creates. Imagine every time you rode an Uber, you earned equity in the company. That's a small example of what crypto rails make possible. On Ethereum, you can access loans from a decentralized protocol with just an internet connection, not gated by income, ethnicity, or social status. With DePIN systems like Helium, I am both a consumer and owner in the mobile network I use.

While building the wiki, it dawned on me that becoming a better programmer, but also helping more projects in the ecosystem, would help scale my impact faster. I tried a coding bootcamp called Hack Reactor in San Francisco, and breathed code 14 hours a day, Monday to Sunday. Then I dropped out.

Dejected, I aimlessly searched for a role for the next six months. By good fortune, mutual connections put me in touch with venture capitalist Tim Draper, and I joined him in investing in early-stage projects. I had respected him for being one of the first prominent figures to lend credibility to the space as early as 2014, and wanted to learn everything I could from him. We invested in early in projects like Arkham, Polymarket, Polygon, and Stacks, some of which would rise a hundredfold in a mere couple of years. Others, like Coinbase or Ledger, would continue to grow and become indispensable for the ecosystem. Interestingly, I felt simultaneously more involved and more left on the sidelines - a contradiction, I know. I missed being a player on the field. On nights and weekends, my computer would click-clack with attempts to get better at code-writing.

Near the end of my time there, Tim asked me if I wanted to raise a crypto fund together that I could manage. By then, crypto had become mainstream, and big-name funds were reaching out, offering high six figure salaries to run crypto for them as a partner. It was very enticing. But I remembered the people I had let down, not least of all myself, in quitting the coding bootcamp. I didn‚Äôt want to leave that ark unfinished.

I redid the bootcamp and graduated, after which some old friends convinced me to co-found a startup with them. One had sold his last startup to Bitgo, and was itching to do something new. I'd happily raise the funding, but on one condition - that I would work as a full-time software engineer.

We initially built a marketplace connecting crypto gaming guilds to players that quickly amassed tens of thousands of players and all the top guilds like YGG and Ancient8. But the market turned, and the guilds went out of business, along with the players. After an unsuccessful pivot to a NFT events platform, we were out of energy, and felt we had one shot left. Having personally lost bitcoin to institutions that crashed in 2022, I hoped to build a product that could help people safeguard their crypto assets. This goes back to how the first cryptocurrency, Bitcoin, was intended to be stored: self-custodied, seizure-proof and censorship-resistant. I also saw that the Gnosis Safe multisig protocol was growing rapidly. That was how our multi-signature crypto wallet got started.

We built a prototype in one month and won 1st place from over 100 projects in a hackathon hosted by Safe, Coinbase and Stripe. This set us on our way. We got to a point where customers were excited about what we were building and had to decide whether to serve retail or enterprise users. I was so proud of all we had gone through together, but this was not where my passion lied. I made the difficult choice to step back, freeing up some equity for the team to hire others more passionate about the direction, and serve in an advisory capacity today, rooting for the team to fulfill their vision. Code

Nest Wallet

A multi-signature crypto wallet that can be secured through multiple devices (mobile, browser extension, ledger). 1st place from over 100 projects in the 1-month long Safe hackathon cohosted by Coinbase and Stripe.

Features: The multisig wallet could connect to any dapp, similar to Metamask, through the Nest browser extension. It was built on top of the Safe protocol, which custodies over $100 billion in assets, and incorporated best-in-class UX with in-app security checks, swap and other transaction capabilities. You can also launch both individual and multisig wallets on dozens of EVM chains with a few clicks from the mobile app.



CryptoPanda

Task: A cryptocurrency price tracker built for my 2-day MVP project in Hack Reactor. Built using Coingecko's API.

Build: React, PostgreSQL, Node.js, Express.

Note that "Comments" section does not work because github-pages (how this is deployed) does not allow hosting of database.



Backend System Design

Task: Built a backend API that could scale to web server level. Achieved 3000 requests / second and sub 50ms latency.

Learnings: Using secondary indexes to optimize table lookup speeds from O(N) to O(logN), horizontally scaling servers with a load balancer and using a ELT process to load millions of lines of csv data into my table schema. Limitations were having to use free-tier t2 micro instances on AWS.

The trickiest part was deploying, since there are so many finnicky parts in dev ops.

Build: NGINX, AWS EC2, PostgreSQL, loader.io (deployed testing) and k6 (local testing).



Project ECOM (Front-end)

Task: Ecommerce website built following detailed client specs. Built with three other engineers in agile sprint environment. I made the "related products" section.

Learnings: Using promise.all to make concurrent API calls, using local storage for storing user styles, and more CSS!

Build: React, Express, Node.js, Amazon EC2.
